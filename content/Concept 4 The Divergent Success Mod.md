Concept 4: The Divergent Success Model

Series: The Age of Intent

*Domain: Political Philosophy & Tech Policy*

*Note: This is explicitly an opinion piece, not a scientific article. It
is an argued position based on structural analysis.*

The Original Idea --- In the Author\'s Own Words

It\'s more of an opinion piece about the differences between the USA and
the European Union --- how they might seem similar at the surface, but
they both have different definitions of success basically.

So if you say an American, an Arabian and a Polish guy walk into a bar
and they each tell each other that they live in the greatest country in
the world --- logically you could say only one of them can be right, but
you can also argue that logically they are all right. They just use
different success metrics.

The guy from Saudi Arabia might value his religion as the basis: they
are so everyone is following Islam, it\'s so important, it\'s so great,
I couldn\'t live in any other country in the world except my own
country. Americans might say: I\'m so free, nowhere in the world am I so
free as here, I live in the greatest country in the world. And same
thing for the Polish guy.

Which is true of course because governments always optimize for whatever
success metric their population gives them. And looking inside from the
outside, societies might seem strange to us or stupid or like they
don\'t know what they\'re doing. We should always assume instead that
governments are fully optimized --- they just have different success
factors.

Looking at the European Union and American population: I presume the
American population tells its governments to facilitate a road to
tremendous wealth and success the most easily. And a European might look
at that and think: but there are so many casualties along the way. On
average, Americans are very poor and lead miserable lives.

The European Union, in fact, instead focuses on the average citizen and
tries to make that one\'s life as happy and as good as possible. So
entirely different success metrics.

And if you continue on that chain of thoughts to how laws are enacted
--- the European Union of course values regulation a lot because
there\'s a lot of Consumer Protection, because that\'s what the citizens
demand. And at huge and enormous costs to businesses of course. And
Americans might see this as inefficiency. They might seek to help
Europeans understand that if they want to run successful businesses and
successful countries, they must assist governments to deregulate. Which
is of course not the correct approach.

Neither is it, from a European point of view, appropriate to criticize
Americans for having such loose gun laws or having a miserable
population. No --- they value independence and the individual strength
of their citizens highly. And they give them every chance to better
their own situations. And they do. So very different success metrics.

Looking at how AI is being implemented in both countries, you see
especially a great divergence in privacy law. While both the United
States and the European Union can be considered Western countries, the
United States wants to give its companies every chance to succeed and
deregulate as much as possible, while the European Union wants to
protect its citizens through regulation. And this is an inherent
conflict that will always come up when dealing with Europe as an
American, or the other way around.

There have been many attempts for cooperation because of course, because
of the shared values, American products are valued highly in the
European Union and the European Union has always been very willing to
pay for American products --- like digital products for example, or AI
models. But they do of course demand them to adhere to their own laws.

Looking at privacy protection right now, we have the Data Protection
Framework, but this is a subject that is always the source of tension
between the countries. And it\'s just a ticking time bomb in my opinion.
The Data Protection Framework will be shut down or proven ineffective
one way or another in a few years. And it might be replaced with an even
stricter or a different model to make sure that American products from
the USA can be sold in Europe. But the structural problem remains.

In my opinion --- and this is why this is an opinion piece and not a
scientific article --- the best way to tackle this is to make products
or services that are entirely European and can operate wholly and with
full sovereignty within the EU sphere. So outside of American possible
wiretaps etc. That\'s the only real guarantee that satisfies the
European Union\'s demand for greater privacy protection. And I think
companies that discover and cater to this need will be most successful.

Core Thesis

Technology is not culturally neutral. The tension between the USA and EU
regarding technology isn\'t just about \"red tape\" or \"innovation\"
--- it\'s a fundamental disagreement on what a successful society looks
like. Therefore, the only way to truly satisfy European values is to
build a fully sovereign European tech stack from the ground up.

The Central Insight: The \"Bar\" Thought Experiment

The \"Polish Guy in a Bar\" paradox: An American, an Arab, and a Polish
person all sit at a bar. Each says they live in the greatest country in
the world. Logically, only one can be right --- but actually, they can
all be right. They are simply measuring against different success
metrics.

This is the foundation of the entire concept: the assumption that any
society that looks strange or irrational from the outside is actually
fully optimized --- it is just optimized for a different goal than the
observer is used to.

This applies universally: Saudi Arabian society, American society,
European society. What looks like dysfunction from the outside is often
optimal function for a different internal metric.

Key Pillars

Pillar 1: Success Metrics as Optimization Goals

The American Metric: Individual wealth, extreme freedom, and
\"high-ceiling\" success. The system is designed to give every
individual the maximum opportunity to reach extraordinary outcomes. The
billionaire is the ideal. The path is hard; many fail; some
spectacularly succeed. The system considers this acceptable because the
ceiling is unlimited.

The European Metric: Collective stability, consumer protection, and
\"high-floor\" success. The system is designed to ensure that the
average citizen has a dignified, comfortable life. Extraordinary wealth
is less the goal; preventing poverty, providing healthcare, and ensuring
security is the goal. The floor is high; the ceiling is somewhat capped
by regulation and taxation.

Neither is objectively better. They serve different populations with
different expressed priorities.

Pillar 2: The Regulatory \"Inefficiency\" Paradox

What Americans call \"inefficiency\" or \"friction,\" Europeans call
\"safety\" and \"civil rights.\"

From an American perspective: EU regulation slows innovation, burdens
businesses, and prevents companies from reaching their potential. The
instinct is to help Europe \"see\" that deregulation leads to better
outcomes.

From a European perspective: US deregulation produces massive
inequality, citizen exploitation, and social instability. The instinct
is to see American companies as predatory.

The insight: both perspectives are correct within their own framework.
You cannot \"solve\" this with a better legal framework or more
dialogue. It is a structural difference in values that will produce
conflict whenever the systems interact.

Pillar 3: The Data Privacy \"Time Bomb\"

Current transfer agreements (like the EU-US Data Privacy Framework, the
successor to Privacy Shield) are temporary bandages.

The structural incompatibility: US law allows --- and in some cases
requires --- bulk surveillance of digital communications for national
security purposes. EU law treats privacy as a fundamental human right.
These two positions cannot be simultaneously satisfied.

The Data Privacy Framework is a diplomatic patch over a legal and
philosophical chasm. As long as US law allows bulk surveillance (Logic
of Defense) and EU law mandates privacy (Logic of the Individual), they
are structurally incompatible.

Prediction: The Data Protection Framework will be shut down or proven
ineffective one way or another in a few years. A \"Schrems III\"
challenge or similar legal action will expose the fundamental
contradiction again.

Historical note: The EU General Court upheld the framework in late 2025,
but privacy advocates including Max Schrems are already preparing
challenges, and the fundamental tension remains unresolved.

Pillar 4: The Case for European Digital Sovereignty

Because American products are legally bound by US law (including
surveillance requirements), no amount of contractual \"compliance\" or
\"privacy shields\" can truly satisfy EU privacy requirements. The
product might promise to be good --- but the underlying legal
architecture requires it to be potentially accessible to US
intelligence.

The only real solution: products and services that are entirely European
and can operate wholly and with full sovereignty within the EU sphere.
This means:

-   EU-owned and EU-operated cloud infrastructure

-   EU-developed AI models trained on EU data

-   Legal architectures that place all data processing within EU
    jurisdiction

-   Physical infrastructure that cannot be subject to US FISA orders or
    National Security Letters

This is not anti-American. It is the logical consequence of the
divergent success metrics. European citizens, expressing their values
through their political system, have defined privacy as a right. The
only way to honor that is sovereign infrastructure.

Pillar 5: The Market Opportunity

Companies that discover and cater to the \"Sovereign-First\" need will
be the ultimate winners in the European market.

This is not just a compliance story --- it is a business story. The
European market is large, wealthy, and has consistently demonstrated
willingness to pay for products that align with its values. A fully
sovereign European AI stack, cloud provider, or enterprise software
suite is not a niche product --- it is a strategically essential one.

The companies that build for this market, designing sovereignty in from
the start rather than bolting on compliance as an afterthought, will
have a sustainable competitive moat that American competitors
structurally cannot replicate.

AI as the Flashpoint

AI has become the sharpest point of this structural conflict because AI
requires:

-   Massive data collection --- which the EU strictly regulates

-   Cloud infrastructure --- which American companies dominate

-   Trained models --- which encode the biases and knowledge of their
    training data

-   Continuous data flows --- which cross jurisdictions constantly

Each of these dimensions is a potential point of US-EU legal conflict.
The EU AI Act, GDPR, and the Data Privacy Framework all represent the
EU\'s attempt to impose its success metrics on the AI layer --- and
American companies\' lobbying against these regulations represents the
US\'s attempt to apply its own metrics.

Tone and Framing Notes

This article should explicitly acknowledge that it is an opinion piece,
not a scientific study.

It must avoid falling into the trap of criticizing either system. The
entire premise is that both are rational --- just optimized for
different things. The analysis is structural, not moral.

The strongest version of this piece treats the American and European
approaches with equal respect and equal analytical rigor, then draws the
conclusion that their incompatibility is not a failure of either --- it
is a structural reality that demands a structural solution.

Connection to the Golden Thread

The abstraction: Intent \> Artifact. The goal: Recognizing that
different cultures have different intents (success metrics) and need
different tools.

Technology is not neutral execution --- it encodes the intent of its
creators and the legal system they operate within. European citizens
demanding privacy are, in the language of the Age of Intent, demanding
that the systems they use reflect their values. American companies
optimizing for growth are reflecting theirs.

The conflict is not about technology. It is about whose intent the
technology serves.
