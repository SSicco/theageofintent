Concept 2: The Strategic Probability Engine

Series: The Age of Intent

*Domain: Geopolitics & Forecasting*

The Original Idea --- In the Author\'s Own Words

The core principle for this is the same principle as using a Monte Carlo
simulation to calculate pi. If you take a square and drop random points
in it, you can calculate using the Pythagorean theorem if the dot falls
in a circle with a radius exactly half of one of the sides of the
square.

To predict the future, you can create a wargame about the situation you
want to explore. Like \"what will happen if the USA withdraws from NATO
tomorrow?\" You assign various factions (countries) and let an AI play
them and let them define actions. Then an arbiter makes a secret roll
(how to do this exactly is a whole other subject we may cover
separately, but the goal is to create a simulation that is as real as
possible) and the timeline is played out until the defined end, which
could be a new stable situation or just a period in time (5 years for
example).

Now running this wargame and doing it well takes a lot of compute. Every
country or faction must define its own strategy, define and evaluate all
of its actions, etc. But even so, say it takes 50 euros to run one
single simulation. The outcome could be defined in multiple dimensions,
or just one. Sticking with the example of the USA withdrawing from NATO,
you could take European relative power, USA relative power and Russian
relative power. If you run this simulation 30 or 50 times, you start to
see the pattern. Maybe in most cases the USA withdrawing might result in
more EU unity and thus power. Or across the majority of simulations, it
might result in the EU fracturing. Or even in a few simulations we have
nuclear war in some form.

This is very valuable information because you not only know what might
possibly happen, but also WHY. Maybe the Russian regime gets cornered so
much that firing nukes is the only logical option left for them.

You can also use this to reconstruct the past. If you enter into the
simulation what was going on at a particular moment in history, you can
run the simulation as well and across multiple simulations that reflect
the historical outcome, really understand WHY generals, kings or people
of a certain period acted like they did. It also allows you to explore
what-if scenarios, or identify moments in history where one side might
have been extremely lucky, or the reverse --- when moments that are
considered lucky actually are not and the outcome would be the same
across simulations.

This idea is not new and the field of wargaming is using this principle
to do planning operations and to understand the other side. Now with AI,
you can not only run even hundreds of wargaming events in parallel, you
can also define it much more easily. You can write a program for example
that scours the current news and runs short simulations regarding
current developments.

A program like this is very bad at low-information events (national
security info is not being shared with the public), or events that rely
heavily on luck or unknown events (predicting who might win the next
world cup for example). It is much better at determining what the best
choice for a faction is at a certain time. What is the most logical
thing for this faction to do given their goals and situation? We as
humans are often bad at placing ourselves in other people\'s shoes and
this simplifies this.

A possible application could be national security or other fields that
are low-intelligence, especially if one faction that is keeping its info
secret inputs it into the simulation and inserts assessments about the
other factions into the model as well. You can even run worst-case
scenario analysis then. (We don\'t know how many nukes Russia has. If we
assume the worst, how will the scenario play out?)

Core Thesis

By treating historical and future events as a series of \"wargame\"
simulations powered by AI agents, we can move from \"guessing\" the
future to \"calculating the probability\" of various timelines. This is
essentially a \"Digital Twin of Geopolitics.\"

While Monte Carlo is traditionally used for physical systems or
financial markets, applying it to high-level human decision-making via
LLMs is a leap into Large World Models.

The Monte Carlo Foundation --- Explained

The Monte Carlo method works by running a large number of random trials
to approximate a result that is too complex to calculate directly. The
classic example: estimating pi.

-   Draw a square. Inscribe a circle within it.

-   Drop random points inside the square.

-   Count what fraction land inside the circle.

-   That fraction approximates pi/4.

The more points you drop, the more accurate the approximation. The same
principle applies to geopolitical simulation: run enough parallel
wargames, and the distribution of outcomes reveals the true probability
landscape.

Key Pillars

Pillar 1: The Agentic Faction Model

Every player (USA, EU, Russia, etc.) is an independent AI agent prompted
with specific goals, internal cultural logic, and resource constraints.

Instead of a single model predicting an outcome, you deploy independent
AI agents representing factions (nations, corporations, revolutionary
groups). Each agent is prompted with that faction\'s specific goals,
constraints, and internal logic.

This is critical: the agents must be grounded in the specific strategic
cultures and literature of the regions they represent. An AI agent
playing Russia cannot simply be a Western-trained model imagining how it
would behave if it were Russian --- it must be shaped by Russian
strategic doctrine, historical priorities, and perceived threat
matrices.

Pillar 2: Stochastic Adjudication --- The Arbiter

To mimic the \"luck\" of real life (e.g., a leader\'s health, a failed
missile launch), a neutral AI \"Arbiter\" processes the moves and
introduces random variables (the \"secret roll\"), ensuring the
simulation isn\'t purely deterministic.

The Arbiter is the simulation\'s referee and physics engine. It enforces
the rules of geopolitical reality --- resource constraints, geographic
limitations, the fog of war --- and introduces the stochastic elements
that prevent any simulation from being purely a chess game of logical
moves.

How to design the Arbiter correctly is noted as a subject that deserves
its own dedicated exploration.

Pillar 3: Convergence Analysis

Running the same scenario (e.g., \"USA leaves NATO\") 50-100 times. If
the EU fractures in 80% of runs, that is a High Probability Outcome. If
nuclear escalation happens in 2%, that is a Tail Risk.

The outcome space can be defined in multiple dimensions simultaneously.
For a NATO withdrawal scenario, you might track:

-   European relative power (rises, falls, fractures)

-   USA relative power (global standing, economic impact)

-   Russian relative power (gains, overextends, collapses)

-   Escalation risk (conventional conflict, nuclear exchange)

The distribution of outcomes across all these dimensions simultaneously
gives a richer picture than any single forecast.

Pillar 4: The Historical \"Why\"

By re-playing history (e.g., the 1914 escalation), we can see if the
real outcome was \"logical\" or a \"statistical anomaly\" driven by
extreme luck. This uncovers the hidden reasoning of historical figures.

History is often a \"sample size of one.\" We look at what happened and
treat it as inevitable. The simulation corrects this: if you re-run July
1914 one hundred times and World War I breaks out in only 12% of
simulations, that tells you something profound about how much of that
catastrophe was contingent rather than inevitable.

Conversely, moments considered \"miraculous\" reversals may turn out to
be highly probable across simulations --- meaning the \"miracle\" was
actually the logical outcome, and our surprise reflects our own
analytical failure.

Pillar 5: The \"Why\" vs. the \"What\"

Because each AI agent\'s \"thought process\" is logged, users can trace
the chain of logic. It\'s not just that Russia fired a nuke, but the
specific sequence of perceived threats that led to that \"logical\"
conclusion.

This is the feature that distinguishes this approach from traditional
forecasting. A prediction model tells you \"X will probably happen.\"
The Strategic Probability Engine tells you: \"X will probably happen
because Actor A will feel threatened by Y, which will cause them to
escalate to Z, which will leave Actor B with no rational option except
nuclear deterrence.\"

The chain of causation is visible and auditable.

Pillar 6: Low-Intelligence Optimization

Even without secret data, the model is excellent at identifying the most
logical next step for a faction based on their public situation. This
makes it immediately useful even in open-source intelligence contexts.

We as humans are often bad at placing ourselves in other people\'s
shoes. The simulation forces rigorous perspective-taking --- each agent
must act according to its own logic, not what an outside observer thinks
is rational.

Applications

Future Prediction

Run forward-looking scenarios on current geopolitical developments. What
happens if X country does Y? The output is not a single prediction but a
probability distribution of outcomes with causal explanations.

Historical Reconstruction

Re-run historical moments to understand whether the outcome was
inevitable or contingent. Useful for academic history, military doctrine
development, and understanding the true sources of historical turning
points.

What-If Scenarios

Explore counterfactual history. What if Germany had not invaded the
Soviet Union in 1941? What if the Cuban Missile Crisis had gone
differently? These scenarios illuminate the structural forces that shape
history beyond individual decisions.

Worst-Case Analysis

When key variables are unknown (e.g., the actual size of a country\'s
nuclear arsenal), run the simulation across a spectrum of assumptions.
\"We don\'t know how many nukes Russia has. If we assume the worst, how
does the scenario play out?\" This provides robust policy planning even
under deep uncertainty.

News-to-Sim Pipeline

An automated system that scours current news and runs short simulations
on daily geopolitical developments. This provides a continuously updated
probability landscape of current events --- a living strategic
intelligence tool.

National Security Applications

Especially valuable in low-intelligence environments. A faction with
classified information about itself can input that into the simulation
privately while using public assessments of other factions. The
simulation then reveals the optimal strategy given the true information
asymmetry.

Constraints and Limitations

The Information Gap

The simulation is only as good as the input. In national security, the
\"Known Unknowns\" are the killers. However, the Worst-Case Analysis
approach is the solution: you don\'t need to know the secret data; you
just run simulations across a spectrum of possibilities.

The \"Echo Chamber\" Risk

If the AI models are all trained on the same Western data, their
\"Russian\" or \"Chinese\" agents might just act like Westerners
imagining they are Russian. To make this work, the agents would need to
be grounded in the specific strategic cultures and literature of those
regions.

Where It Works Best vs. Worst

Best at: Determining the most logical next step for a faction given
their goals and public situation. Long-term structural outcomes.
Identifying causal chains.

Worst at: Low-information events where national security data is not
public. Events that rely heavily on individual luck or unknown variables
(e.g., predicting a World Cup winner).

Computational Cost

Running this well takes significant compute. Every faction must define
its strategy, evaluate all actions, etc. An estimated cost of \~50 euros
per simulation run was noted. However, for a corporation or a
government, 5,000 euros to see 100 versions of a potential war is the
cheapest insurance policy in history.

Future Development Notes

-   Arbiter design: How to create the adjudication system that
    introduces realistic stochasticity without becoming either too
    random or too deterministic.

-   Cultural grounding of agents: How to ensure agents authentically
    represent non-Western strategic cultures.

-   Worst-Case Analysis framework: Standardizing how unknown variables
    are parameterized across simulations.

-   News-to-Sim Pipeline: Technical architecture for automated
    simulation triggered by news events.

-   Validation methodology: How do you validate a simulation? Historical
    backtesting is one approach --- run it on known history and measure
    how often it reproduces the actual outcome.

Connection to the Golden Thread

This concept reflects the same shift from Execution to Intent as the
other concepts in the series. Historical Facts are the \"Substance,\"
and Strategic Personas/Goals are the \"Logic.\"

The abstraction: Logic \> Event. The goal: Predicting \"Tail Risks\" and
Historical \"Whys.\"

We no longer study the timeline (the execution); we study the reasoning
engine (the intent of the actors). If you know how a faction reasons,
you can predict their behavior across all possible timelines, not just
the one we happen to live in.

This moves AI from a \"chatbot\" to a \"Simulated Reasoning Engine\" ---
a tool for understanding the deep logic of human decision-making at
civilizational scale.
